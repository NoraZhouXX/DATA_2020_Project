1 Introduction

In this lab, we will study a classic causal inference question:

Does raising the minimum wage reduce employment?

We will use a fast-food restaurant dataset modeled after the well-known Card and Krueger setting: - New Jersey (NJ) increased its minimum wage - Pennsylvania (PA) did not

This gives us a natural treatment and control setting:

  • Treatment group: New Jersey restaurants
  • Control group: Pennsylvania restaurants
  • Before period: before the policy change
  • After period: after the policy change

Our goals are:

  1. Understand the logic of Difference-in-Differences (DiD)
  2. Compare employment before and after the policy
  3. Compare wages before and after the policy
  4. Estimate the causal effect of the minimum wage increase on:
    • employment
    • wages

3.2 Key Variables for DiD

store_id: unique restaurant identifier state: NJ or PA time: Before or After fte: employment measured as full-time equivalent workers wage: hourly wage chain: restaurant chain treat: indicator for treatment group post: indicator for after period did: interaction term

3.3 Critical Question:

Why do we need both a treatment group and a control group? Why is it not enough to only look at New Jersey before and after the policy?

If we only looked at New Jersey before and after the policy, we would not know whether any employment change was caused by the minimum wage increase or by something else happening over time. For example, employment could change because of seasonal demand, local economic conditions, or industry trends. The control group gives us a benchmark for what might have happened even without the policy.

4.1 Critical Question:

Why is it important to check for missing values before running a regression?

If important variables have missing values, R may silently remove some rows from the analysis. This changes the sample size and can affect results. If the missing data are systematic rather than random, the regression results may also become biased.

5 Create the DiD variables

Even though the dataset already includes treat, post, and did, it is good practice to recreate them ourselves. This helps us understand how the design works.

In a DiD setup: treat = 1 if the restaurant is in NJ, otherwise 0 post = 1 if the observation is after the policy, otherwise 0 did = treat * post

# Recreate treatment indicator: 1 for NJ, 0 for PA
fastfood$treat_new <- ifelse(fastfood$state == "NJ", 1, 0)

# Recreate post indicator: 1 for After, 0 for Before
fastfood$post_new <- ifelse(fastfood$time == "After", 1, 0)

# Create the DiD interaction term
fastfood$did_new <- fastfood$treat_new * fastfood$post_new

9 Part 5: Manual DiD Calculation for Employment

9.1 Why are we doing this?

Before running a regression, we can calculate the DiD estimate manually using the group means. This helps us understand the logic of the DiD estimator. The formula is: DiD = (Y_NJ,After - Y_NJ,Before) - (Y_PA,After - Y_PA,Before)

# Compute average employment in each group
employment_means <- fastfood %>%
  group_by(state, time) %>%
  summarise(mean_fte = mean(fte), .groups = "drop")

employment_means
# A tibble: 4 × 3

  <fct> <fct>     <dbl>
1 NJ    After      20.7
2 NJ    Before     20.1
3 PA    After      19.4
4 PA    Before     19.8
# Extract the four means needed for DiD
nj_before_fte <- employment_means %>%
  filter(state == "NJ", time == "Before") %>%
  pull(mean_fte)

nj_after_fte <- employment_means %>%
  filter(state == "NJ", time == "After") %>%
  pull(mean_fte)

pa_before_fte <- employment_means %>%
  filter(state == "PA", time == "Before") %>%
  pull(mean_fte)

pa_after_fte <- employment_means %>%
  filter(state == "PA", time == "After") %>%
  pull(mean_fte)

# Compute within-state changes
nj_change_fte <- nj_after_fte - nj_before_fte
pa_change_fte <- pa_after_fte - pa_before_fte

# Compute the DiD estimate
did_fte_manual <- nj_change_fte - pa_change_fte

9.2 Critical Question:

Why do we subtract PA’s change from NJ’s change?

Because PA represents the untreated comparison group. Its change over time captures general time trends that may have affected employment regardless of the policy. By subtracting PA’s change, we isolate the extra change associated with treatment.

10.1 Critical Question:

Suppose wage DiD is clearly positive, but employment DiD is close to zero or positive. What does that suggest?

It suggests that the minimum wage increase raised wages without causing a corresponding decrease in employment. This would go against the simplest version of the standard competitive labor market prediction.

11 Part 7: Regression-Based DiD Estimation for Employment

The manual DiD estimate is useful, but regression gives us a standard, formal framework. The formula is: Y = β0 + β1treat + β2post + β3*did + ε

Where: - Y is the outcome (employment or wages) - treat is the treatment group indicator - post is the after period indicator - did is the interaction term (treat * post)

# Run the DiD regression for employment
model_fte <- lm(fte ~ treat_new + post_new + did_new, data = fastfood)

# Show regression output

12.1 Critical Question:

Why is it useful to estimate the wage effect as well as the employment effect?

Because if the minimum wage policy truly changed labor market conditions, we should first see evidence that wages increased in the treated state. Wage results help verify that the treatment actually affected the intended variable. If wages did not change, then we would not expect to see any employment effect either.

Adding controls can help improve the precision of our estimates and account for any differences in chain distribution between states. For example, if NJ has more McDonald’s locations and McDonald’s pays higher wages, that could confound our estimates. By controlling for chain, we can better isolate the effect of the minimum wage policy.

14.2 Critical Question:

Why might controlling for chain be helpful in this setting?

Some chains may systematically pay higher wages or employ more workers than others. If chain composition differs between states, then failing to control for chain could mix treatment effects with chain-specific differences.

15.1 Critical Question:

Why do the manual DiD estimate and the regression interaction coefficient match here?

Because the regression is just a formal way of expressing the same group-mean comparison. In the basic two-group, two-period DiD setup, the interaction coefficient reproduces the same difference-in-differences calculation.

16 Assumptions behind DiD

  • Parallel trends: In the absence of treatment, the average outcomes for treated and control groups would have followed parallel paths over time.
  • No spillovers: The treatment in NJ does not affect outcomes in PA.
  • Stable composition: The set of restaurants in each state does not change systematically over time in a way that is related to the treatment.

In this dataset, we only have one before period and one after period, so we cannot directly test parallel trends well. Because with only one pre-treatment observation, we cannot observe an actual trend before treatment. We can compare levels before treatment, but we cannot see whether the two states were moving similarly over time before the policy.

16.1 Critical Question:

If NJ and PA were already on very different employment trends before treatment, how would that affect our DiD estimate?

Then the DiD estimate might incorrectly attribute those pre-existing trend differences to the policy. In that case, the causal interpretation would be weaker.

17 Conclusions

17.1 Employment

The employment DiD estimate tells us whether employment changed more in NJ than in PA after the policy. If positive: employment rose more in NJ If near zero: employment did not fall relative to PA If negative: employment fell more in NJ ## Wages The wage DiD estimate tells us whether wages changed more in NJ than in PA. If positive: wages rose more in NJ This is what we expect if the policy affected wages

17.2 Critical Question:

Does a positive DiD coefficient automatically prove causality?

Not automatically. It supports a causal interpretation only if the DiD assumptions are credible, especially parallel trends and the absence of major confounding shocks that affected the treated group differently.
